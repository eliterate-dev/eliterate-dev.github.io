I spend a lot of my free time working on my own projects. Most of them are software projects (like this page) - I'm sure I'll present them in a blog post once I'm done with them.

###### This Website

I made this website because I wanted to start a blog, but it quickly turned into a place to just have some fun with (and not enough blogging tbh).

Now I'm tinkering with it regularly, making interesting components (Blazor), building 3D objects and [small games](/toys).

###### My other Projects

My big one at the moment is a platform to present your own projects or just ideas and find people to help you do them - because I needed something like that, so I'm making it.

However because I don't have a convenient site to find help on yet I'm kinda building it alone. It's a "biting off more than I can chew" situation most days, but I enjoy the challenge. 

##### Hardware

Sometimes I like to dabble with microprocessors and IoT stuff too. I've worked with Arduinos and I've done a lot of stuff with various forms of Raspberry Pis, from Pi-holes to hosting Minecraft servers and [Discord bots](https://docs.discord.com/developers/reference).

My favorite piece of hardware is just the [Raspberry Pi Pico](https://shop.pimoroni.com/products/raspberry-pi-pico-2-w) though. It's more of an ESP32 style microcontroller than their other SBC offerings, but it's simple and a lot of fun to tinker with. I use them for silly things like making LED strips flash in pretty colors.

I'm not very good at soldering, but I make do.

I have all the components ready for an Raspberry Pi powered Arcade cabinet, I just need to build and paint it, but I've put that on the back burner for now until my main software project is done.

##### Learning

Saying "learning is a hobby of mine" sounds weird, but it's true. I'm always curious about how things work.

My YouTube watchtime is about an 40/60 split between entertainment and educational content - heavily favouring channels that do both.

I love learning about technologies new and old, from people like <a href="https://www.youtube.com/@TechnologyConnections">Alec Watson</a>, math from people like <a href="https://www.youtube.com/@3blue1brown">Grant Sanderson</a> or <a href="https://www.youtube.com/@standupmaths">Matt Parker</a>, chemistry from <a href="https://www.youtube.com/@NileRed">Nigel Braun</a> or <a href="https://www.youtube.com/@ExplosionsAndFire">Tom de Prinse</a> or what not to do to electronics from <a href="https://www.youtube.com/@ElectroBOOM">Mehdi Sadaghdar</a> and <a href="https://www.youtube.com/@styropyro">Drake Anthony</a>. A lot of those people also come to Open Sauce!

Of course I also read my fair share. When I don't have the focus to concentrate on my eReader, I usually listen to an audio book on train rides or while chilling at home.

I recently listened to [Jon Skeet's "C# in Depth"](https://csharpindepth.com/) as an audio book out of curiosity of how well such a code-dense book would hold up in audio format. It's not something I'd recommend to a beginner, but even without the code examples that a print version would obviously bring, I think it worked decently well. I'll probably get the fifth edition in print, if that ever gets released.
